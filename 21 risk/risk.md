# Risk

So in the end, real risk is not price fluctuation. Risk is the moment when the law of propagation changes. When what, until then, was still being dissipated inside the structure starts getting transmitted instead of absorbed. When the local mechanics stop damping and start amplifying.

At that point, your tools do not just "degrade" gently. Their status changes. Your hedge is no longer a hedge in the same sense. Your diversification is no longer diversification in the same sense. Your execution is no longer execution in the same environment. It is not that your parameters are slightly wrong. It is that the law under which they made sense has changed.

That is exactly why risk never reduces to a variance, nor to a VaR, nor to an isolated stress. All those objects describe possible amplitudes inside a given regime. By themselves, they say almost nothing about the stability of the regime itself.

In other words

**measuring a fluctuation is not equal measuring a regime rupture**

A fluctuation is a move inside a law.

A rupture is the passage into another law.

And that is where almost all serious mistakes are born. Not in "I got the direction wrong." In "I kept thinking with the wrong law."

You can have a very clean portfolio under a regime of slow dissipation. Reasonable correlations, decent execution, depth still present, stable spreads, hedge still feasible. And then the system tips. Depth pulls back. Liquidity stops being continuous. Correlations stop being local and become structural. Forced flows dominate discretionary flows. And then everything that looked separate starts responding to one single common constraint.

That is why, under stress, markets become one-factor. Not because "everyone panics," as some soft phrase. But because one single constraint takes control of the dynamics. Need for cash. Leverage reduction. Margin call. Collateral defense. From that point on, assets are no longer driven by their own fundamentals, nor even by their distinct risk premia. They are driven by the same mechanical necessity.

You can almost write it like this

**stable regime -> multiple responses**

**constrained regime -> single dominant response**

and when that single dominant response takes over, the market loses dimensionality. It no longer "reacts" in a fine-grained way. It executes a constraint.

That is also why the correct reading of risk always starts before price. Price only comes at the end. Before price, there is already liquidity compression, balance-sheet tightening, rising dependencies, deformation in the velocity of circulation, loss of counterparty redundancy. Price only makes visible a change that is already present in the structure.

So if you want to read risk properly, you do not just look at the observed move. You look at the system's capacity to keep absorbing that move without changing its nature.

What does that mean concretely? It means the right variable is not "is it going up or down?" The right variable is: are the absorption mechanisms still holding?

Is there still enough depth to absorb an aggressive flow?

Is there still enough balance-sheet capacity to carry the other side?

Is the hedge still feasible without self-distorting price?

Is funding still available at a bearable cost?

Is the velocity of circulation still compatible with dissipation, or is it starting to propagate the shock faster than it absorbs it?

From there, the "regime-first" logic becomes obvious. You do not try to predict first. You identify the regime first.

Then you adapt the exposure.

**identify the regime -> adapt the exposure**

So the priority is no longer conviction. It is no longer the scenario. It is not even direction in the usual sense. The priority is whether your exposure is compatible with the regime actually in place.

If the system is dissipative, you can carry more inertia, more short convexity, more structure, more finesse in the hedge.

If the system is becoming propagative, the question changes immediately: does your exposure amplify the dominant constraint, or does it remain compatible with it?

And the important sentence here is this one

**past performance is not equal future stability**

A backtest tells you how a strategy behaves in a given world. It contains no guarantee about the point at which that world changes law. It does not see the threshold. It only sees the stretch of history where the threshold has not yet been crossed.

So yes, correlations, ratios, distributions, backtests, sensitivities, all of that remains useful. But only as long as you know in which regime you are reading them. Otherwise you do the most dangerous thing there is: you take objects that are conditional on a regime and treat them as unconditional truths.

And that is exactly where real risk begins. Not when price varies. Not when vol rises. Not when an indicator flashes.

Real risk begins at the moment when the structure stops absorbing and starts propagating. At the moment when the common constraint takes over from local differences, and the moment when the system changes law while you keep reasoning as if nothing had changed.
